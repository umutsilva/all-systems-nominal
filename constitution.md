# All Systems Nominal
## Design Constitution
### Purpose
*All Systems Nominal* is an engineering simulation about constructing, operating, maintaining, and repairing spacecraft in a physically simulated universe. It is **not** a space combat game with construction elements, nor a factory game set in space. It is a simulation of industrial engineering in orbit.
Whenever a design decision must be made, preserving this vision takes priority over adding features.
---
## The Player
The player is an autonomous industrial AI responsible for managing a space shipyard.
The player does not directly manipulate objects. Instead, they issue goals and work orders. Autonomous workers—humans, drones, vehicles, or future machines—carry out those orders according to their capabilities.
The player's role is to design systems, not perform labor.
---
## Core Principles
### 1. One Universe, One Set of Rules
Every object obeys the same physical laws.
Ships, drones, stations, cargo containers, tools, and debris all exist in the same simulation.
There are no special-case objects.
---
### 2. Everything Is Physical
Every component exists somewhere.
Components are manufactured, transported, assembled, removed, repaired, salvaged, and reused.
Nothing teleports.
Nothing appears from nowhere.
Nothing disappears without explanation.
---
### 3. Construction Is Gameplay
Building is not a menu.
Construction is a physical process carried out by workers using real parts in zero gravity.
The ship exists before it is complete.
---
### 4. Engineering Over Micromanagement
The player issues objectives.
Workers determine how to accomplish them.
Good gameplay comes from designing effective infrastructure, workflows, and layouts—not from manually controlling every action.
---
### 5. Automation Is Built
Automation is never unlocked through arbitrary progression.
If the player wants more capability, they must physically build the machines, facilities, and infrastructure that provide it.
Every improvement has an engineering cost.
---
### 6. Incidents, Not Random Events
Factories are imperfect.
Components fail.
Workers make mistakes.
Suppliers deliver defective parts.
Equipment wears out.
Incidents emerge naturally from the simulation.
The game never invents disasters solely to create excitement.
---
### 7. Failure Creates Work
Problems should rarely end the game.
Instead, failures generate new engineering challenges.
Repair.
Recover.
Investigate.
Improve.
Production continues.
---
### 8. Every Machine Is Temporary
There are no permanent "builder units."
Every machine is simply an assembly of components built for a purpose.
If necessary, it can be dismantled and reused elsewhere.
Infrastructure evolves continuously.
---
### 9. Complexity Emerges
Interesting situations should emerge from simple interacting systems.
Whenever possible, replace special-case mechanics with simulation.
Players should discover solutions the designers did not explicitly anticipate.
---
### 10. The Work Never Ends
There is no perfect shipyard.
There is no final optimization.
Every completed project creates the next project.
Progress is measured by capability, not completion.
---
## Forbidden Design Decisions
The following should be avoided unless there is an overwhelming reason otherwise:
* Magical inventories.
* Teleporting resources.
* Global hit points.
* Instant construction.
* Arbitrary technology unlocks.
* Scripted disasters without physical causes.
* Artificial gameplay restrictions that violate the simulation.
* Separate rules for drones, ships, stations, or other machines.
* Features added only because they are common in other games.
---
## Guiding Question
Whenever a new mechanic is proposed, ask:
> **Does this emerge naturally from the existing simulation, or does it require a special rule?**
If it requires a special rule, there is probably a better solution.
---
## Operating Status
Perfection is not the objective.
Continuous operation is.
Incidents are expected.
Repairs are expected.
Adaptation is expected.
**All Systems Nominal.**
